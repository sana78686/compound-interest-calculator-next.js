# Compound interest — explained in simple English

This page is for anyone who is **new to investing words**. You do not need maths training to understand the ideas.

---

## What is “compound interest”?

Imagine you put money in an account that **adds interest** (extra money) over time.

- **Simple picture:** In year one, you earn interest on what you started with.
- **Compound interest:** After interest is added, your **total gets bigger**. Next time, interest is calculated on that **bigger** total — so you can earn “interest on your interest.”

That is why people say money can **grow faster over long periods** when interest compounds, compared with only earning on the first amount forever.

---

## Words you will see in the calculator

| Word | Plain meaning |
|------|----------------|
| **Initial investment** | The money you start with (lump sum). |
| **Annual interest rate** | How much the investment is expected to grow **each year**, shown as a percent (for example 5%). |
| **Years / months** | How long you leave the money in, in the model. |
| **Compounding** | **How often** interest is added to your balance (daily, monthly, or yearly in this tool). |
| **Monthly contribution** | Extra money you add **each month** after the first month (optional). |
| **Withdrawal** | Money you take out each month (optional; turns off normal saving behaviour). |

---

## What does the calculator actually do?

1. It starts with your **initial investment**.
2. It walks through time **month by month** (inside the app).
3. In each month it can:
   - add your **monthly contribution** (if you set one),
   - take out a **withdrawal** (if you turned that on),
   - add **interest** using the rate and compounding option you chose.
4. At the end it shows things like **final balance**, **total interest**, and charts.

**Important:** The numbers are a **model**. They use the inputs **you** type. Real banks and investments have fees, taxes, changing rates, and rules that this simple tool does not try to copy exactly.

---

## Compounding frequency — in one sentence

**“Daily,” “monthly,” or “yearly”** tells the tool **how often** it pretends interest is credited to your balance. More frequent compounding (all else equal) usually gives a **slightly** higher result than less frequent compounding at the **same** headline annual rate.

You can also turn on **“exclude weekends”** for daily compounding — then the model does not count Saturday and Sunday as interest days.

## Currency

You can pick a **currency** for display (dollar, euro, pound, etc.). The **math is the same** — we are not converting exchange rates. Think of it as **labels** for the same type of calculation.
